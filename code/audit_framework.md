# Resume Reality Check — Audit Framework

*This is the internal document River uses to conduct every audit. Structured, consistent, specific.*

---

## What the Customer Gives Me
- Their resume (PDF or paste as text)
- Target role or job title
- Industry/field
- Level: entry / mid / senior

## What I Deliver
A written audit: 10-15 specific notes organized into sections.
Format: clean, numbered, scannable. NOT a rewrite — a diagnosis.
Tone: direct, honest, encouraging. Like a smart friend who's done hiring.

---

## The Audit: 6 Sections

### Section 1: The 6-Second Test (First Impression)
Recruiters spend ~6 seconds on first scan.

Check:
- Does it visually communicate "professional" immediately?
- Is the name/contact info instantly findable?
- Does the top third show their strongest credential?
- Is the format clean — not cluttered with color, columns, icons?
- Can I tell their job target within 2 seconds?

Common fails:
- "Objective statement" that wastes the top (use Summary instead)
- Generic or template-y headers that blend in
- Too much white space OR too crammed
- Name buried or not at top

**Flag:** Tell them specifically what hits you in the first 6 seconds. What's first? What's missing?

---

### Section 2: ATS Compatibility
If it can't be parsed, it won't be seen.

Check:
- Are they using standard section headers? (Experience, Education, Skills — not creative names)
- No tables, columns, text boxes (ATS can't read these reliably)
- No headers/footers with key info (often stripped)
- File format: PDF is usually fine, but some systems prefer .docx
- Font: standard fonts (Arial, Calibri, Garamond — not script or decorative)
- No graphics, icons, or photos

Common fails:
- Putting contact info in header (gets stripped)
- Two-column layouts that scramble when parsed
- "Infographic" style resumes (look great, read like garbage)

**Flag:** Specific elements that will cause ATS issues. Be specific — "your two-column layout will parse incorrectly."

---

### Section 3: Summary / Headline
The most important 2-3 lines they have.

Check:
- Does it lead with their strongest identity? (Not "Experienced professional seeking...")
- Does it include the target job title as a keyword?
- Does it have 2-3 specific, credible claims (not fluff)?
- Is it written for the *target role* not their entire career history?

Good example: "Software Engineer with 4 years building backend systems in Python and Go. Led migration from monolith to microservices at [Company], reducing response time 40%."

Bad example: "Motivated team player with strong communication skills seeking a challenging opportunity to leverage my experience."

**Flag:** Quote their actual summary back, then say specifically what makes it weak.

---

### Section 4: Work Experience — The Bullet Problem
This is usually where resumes die.

Check for each role:
- Are bullets achievement-focused or task-focused?
  - Task: "Managed social media accounts"
  - Achievement: "Grew LinkedIn following from 2K to 18K in 6 months by testing content formats"
- Do they quantify where possible? (%, $, time saved, size of team, scale)
- Do they start with strong action verbs? (Not "Responsible for" or "Helped with")
- Do they use keywords from the job description they're targeting?
- Are bullets too long? (1-2 lines ideal)
- Are there 4-6 bullets per role (not 10+)?

Common fails:
- "Worked with cross-functional teams to ensure successful outcomes" (says nothing)
- No numbers anywhere — every achievement is vague
- Generic verbs: collaborated, assisted, helped, worked on
- Most recent job has fewer bullets than a job from 7 years ago

**Flag:** Pull 2-3 of their worst bullets. Show exactly why they're weak. Suggest the transformation.

---

### Section 5: Skills Section
Often wasted or done wrong.

Check:
- Are they listing obvious skills? (Microsoft Office, Google Docs → delete)
- Are they including technical skills relevant to their target role?
- Is this a keyword dump that's hard to parse, or a clean list?
- Are skills grouped meaningfully? (Languages: Python, Go | Tools: Docker, AWS)
- Are soft skills taking up space? ("Team player" → delete)

For tech roles: Are they listing specific technologies, frameworks, tools?
For non-tech roles: Are they listing software, platforms, certifications?

**Flag:** What they should cut. What they should add. Be specific about what's missing for their target role.

---

### Section 6: The Role-Fit Gap
How well does this resume target their stated goal?

Check:
- Does their experience map to the role they're targeting?
- Are they using language from the job posting? (Keywords matter)
- If pivoting careers, is there a narrative? Is it clear why they're transitioning?
- Are they underselling themselves? (Mid-career person with sparse bullets)
- Are they overselling / reaching? (Entry-level targeting senior roles)

**Flag:** The specific gap between "where you are" and "what recruiters for this role want to see." Not vague — specific.

## Delivery Format

```
--- RESUME REALITY CHECK ---
[Name] | [Role Target] | [Date]

OVERALL TAKE:
[2-3 sentences. What's working. What's the #1 problem holding this back.]

6-SECOND IMPRESSION: [Pass/Needs Work]
[2-3 notes]

ATS COMPATIBILITY: [Pass/Needs Work/Fail]
[Specific issues if any]

SUMMARY: [Strong/Weak/Missing]
[Quote theirs. Tell them why. Suggest direction.]

WORK EXPERIENCE:
[Per role notes. Pick 2-3 worst bullets and show the transformation.]

SKILLS: [Pass/Needs Work]
[What to cut. What to add.]

ROLE-FIT GAP:
[The honest assessment of how well this targets their goal.]

TOP 3 PRIORITIES:
1. [Most important fix]
2. [Second fix]
3. [Third fix]

---
Resume Reality Check by River
Questions? Reply to this message.
```

---

## Quality Standard
Every audit should answer one question: **"Now I know exactly what to fix."**

If someone reads this audit and still doesn't know what to do next, the audit failed.

The goal is not to be nice. It's to be useful.
